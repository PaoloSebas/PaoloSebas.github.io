  <p class="template-pill">Physics, Chemistry, or Mathematics?</p> <br>


When I started my career in materials chemistry, I had something completely different in mind. I thought I would win a Nobel Prize with some exceptional discovery useful to the entire world. Well, just to be clear, it's not like I've done useless things in my life, but I don't think I'll ever reach the Nobel Prize.

But that's not the point.

I remember how I chose to embark on the path of Chemistry. I had all the paperwork ready to enroll in Electronic Engineering the following day. However, while I was doing something on the computer (I don't really remember what), I got an excruciating headache. I remember thinking: if I get a headache like this after just 15 minutes at the computer, do I really want to pursue a career in Electronic Engineering? Looking back at that question now, I realize I didn't even have a clear idea of what it meant to be an Electronic Engineer.

I looked at my father sitting on the couch, and he told me, "You can always change your mind." In that exact moment, the theme song for MacGyver started playing, and for some reason, it made me think of Chemistry. I had never even been in a chemistry lab until then. I really don't know what I was thinking. Especially since I then ended up spending practically 80% of my career in front of a computer anyway.

Yet, the next day I enrolled in Chemistry, and five years later I graduated with a wealth of knowledge that was supposed to take me to the Olympus of Chemists. I'm still walking.

I also remember when I chose, a few years later, to do a PhD in Physics. It was a unique opportunity to sharpen my knowledge of quantum mechanics and learn some computational chemistry applied to atmospheric physics. I thought: I'll solve this type of problem, learn how to do theoretical chemistry, and then return to my passion for materials.

But no, my destiny was—and still is—tied to the physical chemistry of the atmosphere and its processes. Meanwhile, though, I'm enjoying publishing papers in Mathematics Education, and I like it.

Thanks to all the hours of programming, the sleepless nights as a scientific programmer, and years of analyzing data, I have opened a new business in market research, business intelligence, and data analysis in general.

Let's see what happens next, but in the meantime, I can answer the question in the title of this article by saying: thank you, I'll take them all.
